[4] Cash on Cash (CoC), a.k.a. ROI

Cash-on-cash return measures the amount of cash flow relative to the amount of cash invested in a property investment and is calculated on a pre-tax basis.

Typical Method (first year): Focus on cash return based on first year cash flow in relation to cash committed including downpayment and acquisition costs (duties, reno/repair, etc.). Also known as Return of Investment. The higher the better.

[5] Return on Equity (RoE)

RoE measures the income (i.e. cashflow) to net worth (equity is 'net worth' which is market value minus liabilities and includes cash committed, but ignores one-off financial costs) and is calculated on a pre-tax basis.

Method 1 (first year): Cash Flow/Cash initially committed. This is a simple metric to assess 'interest-only' loans or 'full cash' purchase. The higher the better.

Method 2 (end of investment period): Cash Flow ('income') divide by Sales Proceeds ('worth'). The higher the better.

[11] Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

IRR is the tolerable discount rate at which the present value of all expected future cash flows (including sales proceeds from capital gain) matches the initial investment amount (inc. all costs incurred and downpayment). The higher the tolerable discount rate wrt inflation, the better (i.e., preserving value and generates a real growth), but must be higher than hurdle rate. In short, IRR is the rate at which I can tolerate my cash flow to be eroded by time, without losing my purchasing power.

Investment period should be capped to loan tenure since any income after that will be mortgage free, hence requiring a separate calculation.
Note that hurdle rate is the minimum required discount rate to consider an investment viable/ attractive. Therefore, if irr is much higher than fixed deposit rate (opportunity cost), then it's attractive.
